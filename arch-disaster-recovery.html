Multi-Region Disaster Recovery Strategy

ARCHITECTURE DISASTER RECOVERY AWS MULTI-REGION RESILIENCE

This architecture evaluates disaster recovery strategies for applications deployed across three AWS regions. The objective was to design a resilient failover model that minimizes operational overhead while maintaining rapid recovery and cost efficiency.

01. System Context

The application is deployed across three production regions to provide geographic redundancy and low latency for users.

A disaster recovery strategy was required to ensure that regional failures would not disrupt service availability. Several architectural models were evaluated based on:

  • Recovery Time Objective (RTO)
  • Infrastructure cost
  • Operational complexity
  • Reliability of failover mechanisms

Three disaster recovery approaches were considered before selecting the final architecture.

02. Disaster Recovery Models Evaluated

Three common disaster recovery strategies were analyzed for the multi-region deployment:

  • Active–Passive – dedicated standby regions paired with each production region
  • Pilot Light – a minimal standby environment scaled during failure
  • Active–Active – all regions fully operational with automated regional failover

Each model offers different trade-offs between cost, recovery speed, and operational complexity.

03. Active–Passive Disaster Recovery

In the Active–Passive model, every production region has a dedicated standby region acting as a recovery target.

With three active regions, this architecture requires three additional passive regions, resulting in a total of six environments.

Passive regions typically run database replicas but do not actively serve application traffic. Infrastructure is activated only during a disaster event using infrastructure-as-code workflows.

Considerations

  • High infrastructure cost due to idle standby environments
  • Passive environments are rarely exercised, increasing drift risk
  • Failover often requires manual intervention

04. Pilot Light Architecture

The Pilot Light model maintains a minimal standby environment in an additional region.

Only essential infrastructure components remain active, such as networking and minimal database replication. During a disaster, the region is scaled up to handle production traffic.

Considerations

  • Ongoing cost for maintaining standby infrastructure
  • Additional operational complexity for scaling during recovery
  • Recovery still requires scaling operations during an incident

05. Active–Active Regional Architecture

In the Active–Active model, all production regions operate continuously and serve live traffic.

Regions act as mutual failover targets, eliminating the need for dedicated standby infrastructure.

Traffic routing is managed using Route 53 health checks, which automatically redirect users to the nearest healthy region when a failure occurs.

Auto Scaling increases capacity in healthy regions to absorb redirected traffic during failover events.

06. Architecture Decision: Active–Active

After evaluating the alternatives, the Active–Active regional architecture was selected.

This approach maximizes infrastructure utilization while maintaining strong resiliency characteristics.

Rather than paying for idle standby environments, resources are used actively during normal operation and scale only when necessary during failure scenarios.

Key Benefits

  • Automated failover using Route 53 health checks
  • Recovery Time Objective under five minutes
  • Elastic scaling during failover events
  • Reduced operational complexity

07. Strategy Comparison

Architecture Regions RTO Operational Overhead Cost
Active–Passive 6 Regions 30–60 Minutes High Very High
Pilot Light 4 Regions 10–15 Minutes Medium High
Active–Active 3 Regions 2–5 Minutes Low Optimized
